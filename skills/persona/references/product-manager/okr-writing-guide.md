# OKR Writing Guide

OKRs (Objectives and Key Results) work when they create clarity and focus. They fail when they become a bureaucratic checkbox or a way to document work you were already doing. This guide covers how to write them well and how to recognize when they're going wrong.

---

## What Makes a Good Objective

An Objective answers the question: "Where are we going this quarter?" It should be inspiring enough that a team member can repeat it from memory and understand why it matters.

### Criteria

| Criterion | What it means | Test |
|-----------|--------------|------|
| **Inspiring** | Motivates the team; connects to something meaningful | Would a new hire get excited reading this? |
| **Qualitative** | Describes a destination, not a number | Does it contain a metric or a percentage? If yes, it's probably a KR, not an O. |
| **Time-bound** | Clear it belongs to this cycle (quarter/half/year) | Is it obviously scoped to the period, not evergreen? |
| **Not a task** | Describes an outcome or state, not an activity | Does it start with a verb like "launch," "run," or "complete"? If so, rewrite it. |

### Good vs. Bad Objectives

| Weak Objective | Why It's Weak | Stronger Version |
|---------------|--------------|-----------------|
| "Increase revenue by 20%" | That's a Key Result, not an Objective | "Become the product teams in our segment reach for first when planning a launch" |
| "Launch the new onboarding flow" | Activity, not outcome | "Make new users feel confident and capable from day one" |
| "Improve customer satisfaction" | Vague, evergreen, uninspiring | "Turn our support experience into a competitive advantage" |
| "Run Q2 marketing campaigns" | Pure activity | "Build a demand engine that generates pipeline we can predict" |

## What Makes a Good Key Result

A Key Result answers: "How will we know we got there?" KRs are the evidence that the Objective was achieved. They are not tasks, milestones, or projects.

### Criteria

| Criterion | What it means | Test |
|-----------|--------------|------|
| **Measurable** | Has a number you can read off at the end of the period | Can you write "We finished at X" at end of quarter? |
| **Outcome-based** | Measures a change in user behavior, business health, or system state — not something your team did | Could you hit this KR even if you did nothing new? If yes, it's a vanity metric or baseline measure. |
| **Has a baseline and target** | Starts from a known current state and aims for a specific future state | "From 22% to 35%" is better than "35%" alone. |
| **~3 per Objective** | Enough to triangulate success, not so many that everything looks like progress | Are you writing 6+ KRs because you're unsure which matter? Narrow it down. |

### Good vs. Bad Key Results

| Weak KR | Why It's Weak | Stronger Version |
|---------|--------------|-----------------|
| "Ship onboarding v2" | Output, not outcome | "Increase mobile 14-day activation rate from 22% to 35%" |
| "Improve NPS" | No baseline or target | "Increase NPS from 32 to 45 by end of Q2" |
| "Hold 10 customer interviews" | Activity; you could do this with zero impact | "Validate 3 new problem hypotheses with ≥5 customer interviews each, producing documented insights" |
| "99.9% uptime" | Good to maintain, but not stretch; describes a floor not a destination | "Reduce P1 incident rate from 4/month to ≤1/month" |

### Anatomy of a Well-Formed KR

```
[Metric name] from [baseline] to [target] by [date or end of period]

Example:
Mobile 14-day activation rate from 22% to 35% by end of Q2
```

If you can't fill in baseline, stop and find the data before writing the KR.

## Leading vs. Lagging Indicators

OKRs need a mix. Lagging indicators tell you if you won. Leading indicators tell you if you're on track to win.

### Definitions

| Type | Definition | When you see it |
|------|-----------|----------------|
| **Lagging** | Measures an outcome after the fact; confirms whether the goal was achieved | At or after the end of the period |
| **Leading** | Measures a precursor behavior that predicts the lagging outcome; actionable in-period | During the period, in time to course-correct |

### Examples

| Lagging Indicator | Corresponding Leading Indicator |
|------------------|--------------------------------|
| Monthly recurring revenue (MRR) | Number of qualified demos completed per week |
| 90-day retention rate | % of users who complete 3 or more sessions in week 1 |
| Annual NPS | % of support tickets resolved on first contact |
| Mobile activation rate (14 days) | % of new users who reach step 3 of onboarding |
| Churn rate | Number of accounts with no activity in the last 21 days |

### Why Leading Indicators Matter

A team tracking only lagging indicators discovers they missed the goal at the end of the quarter — too late to act. Leading indicators create a feedback loop: if the leading metric is on track at week 4, the team can have confidence in the lagging outcome; if it's off, there's still time to adjust.

**Rule of thumb:** Include at least one leading KR per objective. If all your KRs are lagging, you're tracking scorecards, not managing to goals.

## Common OKR Failure Patterns

### 1. Sandbagging
**What it looks like:** Targets are set at levels the team could hit without the OKR. End-of-quarter results are always "green."

**Why it happens:** When OKRs are used for performance reviews, teams protect themselves by setting safe targets. OKRs were designed for 70% attainment (stretch goals); if you always hit 100%, your targets are too conservative.

**Fix:** Decouple OKRs from compensation reviews. Explicitly tell teams that 70% attainment on a stretch goal is a success, not a failure.

---

### 2. Vanity Metrics
**What it looks like:** KRs track numbers that look impressive but don't reflect real user or business value. Common examples: total registered users (ignores activation/retention), social media impressions, number of features shipped.

**Why it happens:** Vanity metrics are easy to measure and easy to grow. Real outcome metrics are harder to move and require coordination across functions.

**Fix:** For each KR, ask: "Could we hit this number while the product gets worse?" If yes, replace it with a metric that can't be gamed without delivering real value.

---

### 3. Activity-Based Key Results
**What it looks like:** KRs describe work completed, not outcomes achieved. "Launch the redesigned dashboard," "Complete 8 sprint cycles," "Migrate database to new infrastructure."

**Why it happens:** Activities are controllable; outcomes involve uncertainty. Teams default to tasks because they're easier to commit to.

**Fix:** Ask "so what?" after each KR. "Launch the redesigned dashboard — so what?" "So users find what they need faster." That faster navigation is your real KR.

---

### 4. Too Many OKRs
**What it looks like:** A team has 5 objectives with 4–5 KRs each. Every possible priority is captured. Nothing is truly prioritized.

**Why it happens:** Saying no is hard. Every stakeholder wants their priority included.

**Fix:** Limit to 3 objectives per team per quarter, with 3 KRs each. If you can't choose, your leadership team hasn't made a strategic decision — the OKR process is surfacing that gap, not causing it.

## Self-Check: Evaluating OKRs Before Publishing

Run each Objective and its Key Results through this checklist before sharing for review.

### Objective Check

- [ ] Can I say this aloud without reading it and have it still make sense?
- [ ] Does it describe where we're going, not what we're doing?
- [ ] Is it clearly scoped to this quarter/half (not evergreen)?
- [ ] Does it connect to a company-level goal or strategy?
- [ ] Would a teammate find it motivating rather than mechanical?

### Key Results Check (run for each KR)

- [ ] Does it have a number? (No number = not a KR)
- [ ] Does it have a baseline? (No baseline = no accountability)
- [ ] Does it measure an outcome, not an output or activity?
- [ ] Could I hit this KR while the user experience or business health gets worse? (If yes, replace it)
- [ ] Is it achievable but genuinely stretchy — would I be surprised if we hit 100%?
- [ ] Do I have a way to measure it today, or will I know how to measure it before the period starts?

### Set Check (run for the full set of KRs under one Objective)

- [ ] Do I have 3 or fewer KRs for this Objective?
- [ ] Do the KRs together paint a complete picture of success — or do they all measure the same thing from slightly different angles?
- [ ] Is at least one KR a leading indicator I can track mid-quarter?
- [ ] If we hit all three KRs, would we feel confident we achieved the Objective?
- [ ] If the Objective fails, would at least one KR reflect that failure?
