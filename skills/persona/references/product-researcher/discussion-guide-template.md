# Discussion Guide Template

Use this template for both user interviews (generative) and moderated usability tests (evaluative). Adapt sections based on session type. A typical session runs 45–60 minutes.

---

## Session Structure Overview

| Phase | Duration | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| Intro & Consent | 2–3 min | Set context, get recording consent, frame the session |
| Warm-Up | 5–8 min | Build rapport, establish participant context, reduce anxiety |
| Core Questions | 20–35 min | Explore the topic; for usability tests, replace with task scenarios |
| Task Scenarios *(usability only)* | 15–25 min | Observe behavior on defined tasks |
| Debrief | 5–8 min | Surface overall impressions, anything participant wants to add |
| Close | 1–2 min | Thank participant, explain next steps |

---

## Intro Script (Adapt as Needed)

> "Thanks for joining today. I'm [name], and I'll be guiding our conversation. We're talking with people to better understand [general topic — keep vague]. There are no right or wrong answers — we're here to learn from your experience, not to test you.
>
> With your permission, I'd like to record the session so I can focus on our conversation rather than taking notes. The recording is just for our internal research team and won't be shared publicly. Is that okay?"

---

## Warm-Up Questions

The goal is to get the participant comfortable and establish context about who they are before diving into the core topic. Keep this conversational.

1. "Can you start by telling me a bit about what you do day-to-day — professionally or in whatever context is most relevant?"
2. "How long have you been [doing the relevant activity — e.g., managing a team, freelancing, using this type of tool]?"
3. "Walk me through a recent time you had to [relevant activity]. How did that go?"
4. "What tools or approaches do you currently use for [relevant domain]? How did you end up with those?"

---

## Core Questions

### Structuring Good Questions

- Lead with **open-ended prompts**: "Tell me about a time when...", "Walk me through how you...", "What does that look like for you?"
- Avoid binary questions: replace "Do you find X useful?" with "How do you use X, if at all?"
- Sequence from broad → specific: start with behaviors and context before opinions and preferences

### Follow-Up Probe Bank

Use these to go deeper without leading the participant:

| Probe | When to Use |
|---|---|
| "Tell me more about that." | When the answer is brief or vague |
| "What did you expect to happen?" | After they describe a surprise or frustration |
| "Can you walk me through exactly what you did?" | When you need behavioral detail, not summary |
| "What does that mean to you?" | When they use a term or phrase that could mean different things |
| "Why was that important?" | To surface underlying motivation or mental model |
| "What happened next?" | When a story stops before the resolution |
| "You mentioned [X] — can you say more about that?" | To pick up on something important said in passing |
| "Is there anything else you'd want me to know about that?" | Before moving to a new topic |

### Core Question Template (Generative Interview)

```
1. [Contextual opener — understand current state]
   "Walk me through how you currently handle [domain]."

2. [Pain and friction]
   "What's the most frustrating part of that process?"
   Follow-up: "Tell me about a specific time that happened."

3. [Workarounds and alternatives]
   "When [pain point] happens, what do you do about it?"

4. [Goals and motivations]
   "What are you ultimately trying to accomplish when you [activity]?"

5. [Prior experience / comparisons]
   "Have you used anything else to try to solve this? What was that like?"
```

## Task Scenarios (Usability Tests Only)

### Task Scenario Format

Tasks should describe a realistic goal without revealing the UI path to accomplish it.

**Structure:**
> *Context sentence (who they are, what they're trying to do)* + *Goal statement (what done looks like)* — no hints about where to click.

**Example (bad):** "Click the Settings gear and navigate to Notifications to turn off email alerts."

**Example (good):** "Imagine you've been getting too many email notifications from this app. Go ahead and turn those off however you'd like, and let me know when you feel like you've done that."

### Task Administration Tips

- After reading the task: "Go ahead and think out loud as you work through it — just narrate what you're doing and what you're thinking."
- When they get stuck, wait at least 10–15 seconds before asking a neutral probe: "What are you thinking right now?" or "What would you do if this were on your own?"
- Never point, nod, or indicate whether they're on the right track.
- After each task, ask: "How did that feel?" / "Was there anything surprising or confusing there?"

---

## Debrief Questions

Run these after all tasks or core questions are complete.

1. "Thinking about the overall experience today — what stood out most to you, positively or negatively?"
2. "If you could change one thing about [product/topic], what would it be?"
3. "Is there anything I didn't ask about that you think would be useful for me to know?"

---

## Closing

> "That's everything I have. This was really helpful — thank you for your time. We'll use what we learned here to improve [product/experience]. If any other thoughts come to you after we wrap up, feel free to reach out. Is it okay if we contact you with follow-up questions in the future?"

---

## Sidebar: What Not to Say

Leading questions bias responses toward what the researcher (consciously or not) wants to hear. Watch for these patterns:

| Leading Phrasing (Avoid) | Why It's a Problem | Neutral Rephrasing |
|---|---|---|
| "Did you find the checkout process confusing?" | Suggests confusion is the expected response | "What was your experience with the checkout process?" |
| "Don't you think this design is cleaner?" | Yes/no with an implied correct answer | "How does this compare to what you saw before?" |
| "Would it be useful if the button were bigger?" | Participant will usually agree with feature suggestions | "What, if anything, would you change about this?" |
| "Most users find this step frustrating — what do you think?" | Anchors the participant to others' responses | "What was your reaction to this step?" |
| "So you felt overwhelmed by the options?" | Paraphrases with an emotion they didn't use | "It sounds like there was a lot to take in — how would you describe it?" |
